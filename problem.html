The Problem

Pressure injuries remain one of healthcare's most preventable—yet persistent—complications.

Patients who remain seated or bedridden for extended periods experience sustained tissue compression. Over time, this restricts circulation and elevates the risk of tissue breakdown—a process that is largely preventable with the right monitoring tools.

What Are Pressure Injuries?

Pressure injuries—also known as pressure ulcers or bedsores—are localized damage to the skin and underlying tissue, typically occurring over bony prominences. They result from sustained mechanical loading that restricts blood flow, depriving tissue of oxygen and nutrients. The severity ranges from superficial skin redness to deep wounds exposing muscle and bone, with treatment costs reaching tens of thousands of dollars per case.

Why Duration Matters

Research demonstrates that tissue damage is not solely a function of pressure magnitude—duration of exposure plays an equally critical role. Even moderate pressure, when sustained without relief over extended periods, can initiate cellular damage. This time-pressure relationship is fundamental to understanding why repositioning protocols exist, and why real-time monitoring could transform prevention.

Pressure × Duration

The relationship between pressure magnitude and exposure time determines injury risk. High pressure over short periods can be as damaging as moderate pressure sustained over hours. Current clinical guidelines recommend repositioning every two hours, but compliance remains inconsistent across care settings.

The Accessibility Gap

Advanced pressure monitoring systems used in clinical environments can cost thousands of dollars per unit—limiting adoption in underfunded hospitals, rehabilitation centers, long-term care facilities, and home healthcare settings where they are needed most.

Why It Matters

The case for predictive monitoring

Most existing systems respond to injuries after they occur. PressureGuard proposes a shift toward predictive monitoring—continuously analyzing pressure exposure and duration to identify risk before tissue damage begins. By making this technology accessible and affordable, prevention can extend beyond well-funded clinical environments to the communities that need it most.
