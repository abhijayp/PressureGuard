The Problem

Pressure injuries remain one of healthcare's most preventable complications.

Patients who remain seated or bedridden for extended periods experience sustained tissue compression between bone and external surfaces. Over time, prolonged pressure restricts circulation, increases localized stress accumulation, and elevates the risk of tissue breakdown.

Pressure + Duration

Pressure injuries are influenced not only by force magnitude, but also by how long tissue remains compressed without relief. Continuous exposure over time significantly increases injury risk, making temporal tracking essential for early detection.

Accessibility Gap

Advanced monitoring systems used in clinical environments can cost thousands of dollars, limiting adoption in underfunded hospitals, rehabilitation facilities, and home healthcare settings where patients need protection most.

Why It Matters

Existing systems are reactive, not predictive.

Most clinical approaches rely on scheduled manual repositioning or visual skin assessments. By the time tissue damage is detected, it has often already begun. A predictive approach that continuously monitors pressure distribution and exposure duration could intervene before injury occurs.

2.5 million cases annually

Pressure injuries affect approximately 2.5 million patients in the United States each year, contributing to extended hospital stays, increased care costs, and in severe cases, life-threatening complications.

$26.8 billion in annual costs

The financial burden of treating pressure injuries across the U.S. healthcare system is substantial, making prevention not only a clinical priority but also an economic imperative for healthcare institutions.

Manual assessment limitations

Current prevention protocols depend heavily on nursing staff performing visual skin inspections and scheduled repositioning. These methods are labor-intensive, inconsistent, and unable to capture the continuous data needed for early intervention.

Technology cost barriers

Smart bed and mattress overlay systems that do offer continuous monitoring typically cost $10,000 to $30,000 per unit, placing them beyond the reach of most care facilities and entirely inaccessible for home care patients.
